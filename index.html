Reading Group

v3.0 | Owner Luis Holzhauer | Weekly Monday 8am | Remote

Paper-first discussion format for doctoral and master-level research.

This reading group is designed as a disciplined space for reading difficult papers together, documenting reusable ideas, and turning discussion into stronger research habits across the group.

Weekly cadence Remote participation Moderation rotation

Why it exists

Objectives

  • Push ourselves to read papers consistently, including technically demanding ones.
  • Use the group to unpack complex papers that matter for our own work.
  • Screen, challenge, and support interested bachelor, master, and PhD students.
  • Identify and document ideas that can grow into future research projects.
  • Motivate people for Sports Analytics research and related projects.
  • Use the group's knowledge to review almost-ready papers before submission.
  • Strengthen our network, including through interaction with paper authors when possible.

Working style

Core principle

Thorough preparation can be exhausting, especially for technical papers, but the group treats careful reading as a shared investment in scientific growth and stronger collective work.

Participation rules

Expectations

  • Internal PhD students should attend at least 70% of Reading Group sessions.
  • Do not attend a meeting without reading the paper in advance.
  • Every internal PhD student should moderate at least 2 sessions every 3 months.
  • Non-internal PhD students should moderate at least 5 sessions on papers relevant to their PhD over the course of their study.
  • Non-internal PhD students are encouraged to use the Reading Group for papers connected to their research and may choose whether additional sessions are worth attending.
  • Master thesis students should suggest and moderate sessions for at least one paper relevant to their thesis.
  • Because larger groups are harder to steer well, participants should write down thoughts and questions while reading instead of relying on ad hoc discussion.
  • Attendance should be tracked whenever the Reading Group counts toward evaluation or course participation.

What makes a good session

Moderation notes

Sessions work best when moderators frame a few concrete takeaways up front: what problem the paper solves, what assumptions it makes, and what the group should challenge. The point is not only to summarize, but to surface reusable ideas for the institute's own work.

Best fit

Who should use it

The format is especially useful for PhD students, master thesis students, and anyone in the institute who wants a rigorous but collaborative place to process papers before they shape a project, thesis chapter, or submission.
